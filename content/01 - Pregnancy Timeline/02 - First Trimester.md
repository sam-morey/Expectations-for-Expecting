**Weeks 1–13**

The first trimester is the most critical developmental period. All major organ systems form during these weeks. It is also typically the most symptomatic phase for mom — nausea, fatigue, and emotional adjustment are common.

---

## What's Happening with Baby

| Week | Baby Size   | Key Development                                                              |
| ---- | ----------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 4    | Poppy seed  | Implantation complete; hCG rising; pregnancy test positive                   |
| 5    | Sesame seed | Neural tube forming; heart beginning to form                                 |
| 6    | Lentil      | Heartbeat detectable on transvaginal ultrasound (~110–160 bpm)               |
| 7    | Blueberry   | Brain growing rapidly; arm and leg buds appear                               |
| 8    | Kidney bean | All major organs present in rudimentary form; fingers forming                |
| 9    | Grape       | Embryo officially becomes a fetus; tail disappears                           |
| 10   | Kumquat     | Organs functional; heartbeat detectable on Doppler                           |
| 11   | Fig         | Toenails and fingernails forming; baby can open mouth                        |
| 12   | Lime        | Reflexes developing; can yawn and stretch                                    |
| 13   | Peach       | Placenta takes over hormone production; miscarriage risk drops significantly |

---

## What's Happening with Mom

### Physical Symptoms (Common)
- **Nausea / morning sickness** — Often peaks weeks 8–10; can be morning, evening, or all day
- **Fatigue** — Profound; progesterone is high; body building placenta
- **Breast tenderness** — Increased sensitivity and size
- **Frequent urination** — Expanding uterus; hCG effects on kidneys
- **Bloating** — Progesterone slows digestion
- **Food aversions and cravings** — Triggered by hormonal changes and heightened smell
- **Spotting** — Light implantation bleeding around week 4 is normal; heavier bleeding warrants contact with provider
- **Constipation** — Progesterone slows gut motility

### Emotional
- Mood swings driven by rapid hormonal shifts
- Anxiety about miscarriage risk (elevated in first trimester) is normal
- Ambivalence, excitement, and fear can coexist

## Medical Care in the First Trimester

| Test / Event | Timing | What It Is |
|---|---|---|
| Confirmation appointment | Week 8–10 | Confirm pregnancy, EDD, first ultrasound (often transvaginal) |
| Dating ultrasound | Week 7–10 | Confirms gestational age; checks for heartbeat |
| Cell-free DNA (cfDNA) / NIPT | Week 10+ | Screens for chromosomal abnormalities (trisomy 21, 18, 13) |
| Nuchal translucency (NT) scan | Week 11–14 | Ultrasound + blood test; screens for Down syndrome |
| Blood panel | Week 8–10 | Blood type, Rh factor, CBC, STI screen, thyroid, rubella immunity |
| First trimester screening | Week 11–13 | Combined NIPT + NT scan |

## Nutrition Priorities

- **Folic acid / folate:** Critical for neural tube closure (occurs weeks 3–4 — often before confirmed pregnancy)
- **Start prenatal vitamin** with at least 400–800mcg folate if not already taking
- Manage nausea: small, frequent meals; bland foods; ginger; B6 supplementation
- Avoid: raw fish/meat, high-mercury fish, unpasteurized dairy, deli meats, alcohol, excessive caffeine (>200mg/day)

---

## What to Do in the First Trimester

- [ ] Schedule first prenatal appointment
- [ ] Start prenatal vitamin (if not already)
- [ ] Reduce alcohol, caffeine; stop if possible
- [ ] Discuss NIPT / genetic testing options with provider
- [ ] Research OB vs. midwife vs. birth center options
- [ ] Consider when and how to announce
- [ ] Review workplace leave policies
- [ ] Begin researching pediatricians

---

## Miscarriage Risk Context

- Miscarriage risk is highest in weeks 4–8 (~15–20% of confirmed pregnancies)
- After a heartbeat is detected at 8 weeks, risk drops to ~3–5%
- After week 12, risk drops to <1% for chromosomally normal pregnancies
- Most miscarriages are caused by chromosomal abnormalities — not by anything the mother did
