Knowing the difference between prelabor signs, false labor, and true labor reduces unnecessary trips to the hospital — and prevents staying home too long.

---

## Pre-Labor Signs (Days to Weeks Before Labor)

These indicate the body is preparing but labor hasn't begun:

### Lightening
Baby drops lower into the pelvis. You can breathe more easily but have increased pelvic pressure and more frequent urination. Can happen days to weeks before labor in first pregnancies; sometimes only during active labor in subsequent pregnancies.

### Mucus Plug
A thick discharge (clear, pink, or brown-tinged) that sealed the cervix. Loss of the mucus plug indicates the cervix is beginning to soften and open. **This can be days to weeks before labor** — not a signal to rush to the hospital.

### Bloody Show
Mucus tinged with blood (bright red or pink). The cervix is ripening and capillaries are breaking. Often indicates labor within **24–72 hours** but is not a guarantee.

### Nesting Instinct Surge
A sudden, intense need to clean, organize, and prepare. Normal; channel it productively; also rest.

### Loose Stools / GI Changes
Prostaglandins that trigger cervical ripening also stimulate the bowel. Loose stools in the day before labor is common.

### Braxton Hicks Increase
More frequent and sometimes stronger practice contractions. Still irregular, not progressive.

## False Labor vs. True Labor

| Feature | Braxton Hicks / False Labor | True Labor |
|---|---|---|
| Pattern | Irregular; don't follow a pattern | Regular; get closer together over time |
| Intensity | Don't get stronger; may weaken | Progressively stronger |
| Location | Usually front of abdomen | Often starts in lower back; wraps to front |
| With position change | Often resolve with rest, walking, or hydration | Continue regardless of position or activity |
| Cervical change | No dilation | Progressive dilation |

## The 5-1-1 Rule — When to Go

**5 minutes apart, lasting 1 minute each, for 1 hour.**

When contractions meet this pattern consistently, call your provider and head to the hospital or birth center.

**For second or subsequent pregnancies:** Earlier — 7-1-1 or when contractions become strong and regular. Subsequent labors can progress very quickly.

**Modify for your situation:** Your provider may give you specific instructions. Follow their guidance over a general rule.

## Water Breaking (Rupture of Membranes)

The amniotic sac can rupture as a gush (obvious) or a trickle (harder to distinguish from urine or discharge).

**Signs it's amniotic fluid:**
- Clear to pale yellow; slightly sweet smell (not urine)
- Continuous slow leak that doesn't stop (unlike a single burst of urine)
- Cannot be stopped by Kegel squeeze (unlike urine)

**When water breaks:** Call your provider immediately regardless of whether contractions have started. Most women begin labor within 12–24 hours. Provider may recommend coming in immediately depending on GBS status, gestational age, and color of fluid.

**Green or brown-colored fluid (meconium):** Go immediately; may indicate fetal distress.

---

## Go Immediately Without Calling First If:

- Heavy vaginal bleeding (not just bloody show)
- Cord prolapse (cord visible outside vagina)
- Signs of placental abruption: sudden severe abdominal pain + uterus hard and doesn't relax between contractions
- Baby's movement has stopped
- You feel the urge to push (labor may be very advanced)

## Timing Contractions

Use an app (Full Term, Sprout, Ovia) or manual tracking:
- Record start time of each contraction
- Record end time (or duration)
- Note the gap between contractions (start of one to start of next)

Bring this log to the hospital — it helps the care team assess progress.
