Labor is the process by which the uterus contracts to dilate the cervix and eventually expel the baby. Understanding how labor progresses — and what's normal at each stage — reduces fear and improves decision-making.

---

## The Three Stages of Labor

### Stage 1: Cervical Dilation
The uterus contracts, the cervix effaces (thins) and dilates from 0 to 10 centimeters.

**Divided into:**

| Phase | Dilation | Contractions | Duration (first birth) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Early / latent labor | 0–6 cm | 5–30 min apart; 30–60 sec long | Hours to days (variable) |
| Active labor | 6–10 cm | 3–5 min apart; 60–90 sec long | 4–8 hours |
| Transition | 8–10 cm | 2–3 min apart; 90 sec; most intense | 30 min – 2 hours |

**Early labor:** May be spent at home. Contractions are present but manageable. Walk, rest, eat, distract. **Go to the hospital when contractions follow the 5-1-1 rule:** 5 minutes apart, lasting 1 minute, for at least 1 hour.

**Active labor:** This is hospital/birth center time. Contractions are intense and require focus and coping strategies.

**Transition:** The most intense phase — and often the shortest. Many women want to quit at transition; it's nearly over.

### Stage 2: Pushing and Birth
Cervix fully dilated at 10 cm; pushing begins with contractions.

- **Duration:** 20 minutes to 3+ hours (first births typically longer; epidural lengthens)
- Baby moves down and through the birth canal with each push
- Crowning (baby's head visible); then head delivered, then shoulders, then body
- Birth

### Stage 3: Placenta Delivery
After the baby is born, the uterus continues contracting to expel the placenta.

- **Duration:** 5–30 minutes
- Pushing may still be needed
- Provider may apply gentle traction on umbilical cord
- Uterine massage begins to control bleeding
- Any tears or episiotomy repaired (with local anesthetic)

## What "Normal Labor" Feels Like

- **Early contractions:** Feel like strong menstrual cramps or a tightening band around the abdomen and lower back
- **Active contractions:** Intense, usually painful; require full focus; breathing, movement, or epidural for management
- **Transition:** Overwhelming pressure; many women feel they cannot do it; this is the signal that the end is very close
- **Pushing:** Immense pressure; often described as a strong urge to push (like the most intense bowel movement); with epidural, sensation is reduced but pushing instinct may remain

---

## Typical First Labor Timeline

- **Total average first labor:** 12–18 hours (highly variable)
- Active labor alone: 4–8 hours
- **Subsequent labors:** Often faster; active labor 2–5 hours; some precipitous (very fast)

## Pain Management Decisions

Every labor is different. There is no right answer.

| Option | What It Is | Tradeoffs |
|---|---|---|
| Epidural | Regional anesthesia; catheter in epidural space; eliminates most pain | Most effective; requires IV, continuous monitoring; may slow pushing; rare complications |
| Nitrous oxide | Inhaled 50/50 O2/N2O; reduces anxiety; doesn't eliminate pain | Not available at all hospitals; does not provide full pain relief |
| IV opioids (fentanyl, morphine) | Reduces pain intensity; sedating | Crosses placenta; may affect baby's breathing if given close to delivery |
| Unmedicated (natural) | No medication; relies on position, movement, breathing, water | Requires preparation; intense; possible in healthy, low-risk labors |
| Water immersion / tub | Warm water reduces pain perception; relaxes muscles | Very effective for active labor; not always available; not for delivery at all hospitals |

See [[04 - Pain Management Options]] for full detail.

## Induction

Induction means medically starting labor before it begins spontaneously.

**Common reasons:**
- Post-dates (41–42 weeks)
- Gestational hypertension or preeclampsia
- PROM (premature rupture of membranes with no contractions)
- Gestational diabetes with macrosomia concern
- Elective induction at 39+ weeks (evidence supports safety)

**Methods:** Cervical ripening (Misoprostol, Foley balloon, Cervidil) then Pitocin (synthetic oxytocin) via IV.

**Induction contractions** are often described as more intense and less gradual than spontaneous labor contractions — epidural rate is higher with induction.
