# Sleep Training Approaches

Sleep training is one of the most debated parenting decisions — and one that research shows is largely safe regardless of method, provided it is done when developmentally appropriate. The "right" approach is the one that works for your family.

---

## The Basics

**What is sleep training?** Teaching an infant to fall asleep independently — without being nursed, rocked, or held to sleep — so they can also return to sleep independently when they wake between sleep cycles.

**When is it developmentally appropriate?**
- The AAP and most pediatric sleep researchers agree: no earlier than **4 months** (adjusted age for preterm); most commonly done at 4–6+ months
- Before 4 months, babies lack the neurological capacity for sustained independent settling
- Some families wait until 6 months; some until they see signs of readiness

**Is it harmful?**
Multiple RCTs and longitudinal studies have found no evidence of harm to infant attachment, stress hormone levels, or behavioral development from sleep training done at 4 months or later. This includes extinction ("cry it out") methods.

---

## The Main Methods

### Extinction ("Cry It Out" / CIO)
- Parent puts baby down awake; leaves; does not return until morning (or a defined time)
- Baby cries, sometimes significantly, for 1–3 nights; then typically settles
- **Evidence:** Most effective; fastest; no long-term harm shown
- **Challenge:** Emotional difficulty for parents; some parents cannot do it regardless of evidence

### Graduated Extinction (Ferber Method)
- Parent puts baby down awake; leaves; returns after set intervals (progressively longer) to briefly check in — but does not pick up or feed
- Intervals might be 3 min / 5 min / 10 min; then 10 min every time thereafter
- **Evidence:** Effective; slightly slower than extinction; widely used
- **Challenge:** Some research suggests check-ins can extend crying for some babies

### Chair Method (Sleep Lady Shuffle)
- Parent sits next to crib; moves chair toward door over successive nights; eventually out of room
- Fading approach; baby can see parent
- **Evidence:** Less research; works for some families; slower
- **Challenge:** Parent presence can sometimes make it harder (baby escalates to get picked up)

### Fading / Delay
- Gradually delay the sleep association (e.g., slowly reduce rocking time; put down drowsy instead of asleep)
- No crying involved but requires very gradual, structured progression
- Works for mild sleep associations; less effective for strongly habituated behaviors

---

## Sleep Foundations Before Training

Sleep training is more effective when combined with sleep hygiene:
- **Consistent bedtime routine:** Bath → feed → book → song → crib; predictability signals sleep
- **Age-appropriate wake windows:** Tired but not overtired at bedtime
- **Dark, cool room** with white noise
- **Early bedtime:** 6:30–8pm for most infants; later bedtime increases overtiredness
- **Filling the tank:** Full feeds during the day; ensuring nighttime waking is sleep association, not hunger

---

## Night Feedings

Sleep training and night feedings are separate questions.

- Before 6 months: Most babies need 1–2 night feeds; don't expect to eliminate these
- After 6 months: Healthy, growing babies on appropriate solid foods may no longer need night feeds; discuss with pediatrician
- You can address night wakings independently from feeds — some families eliminate all pacifier/settling wakings while preserving one feeding

---

## The Philosophy Question

Some parents feel strongly that responding to every night waking is an extension of responsive parenting. Others believe sleep deprivation harms the whole family and that healthy sleep is itself a gift to the child.

**Both positions contain real values.** The research does not mandate one approach. The family's wellbeing (including parental mental health and sustainability) is a legitimate factor in the decision.
