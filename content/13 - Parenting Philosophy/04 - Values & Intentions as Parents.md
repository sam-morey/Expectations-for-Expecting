Articulating what you believe as parents — before the chaos of a newborn makes reflection impossible — creates an anchor. These aren't rules; they're north stars to return to when decisions are hard.

---

## Why Write This Down

Parenting decisions rarely happen in calm, reflective moments. They happen at 3am, during tantrums, during disagreements with a partner, in waiting rooms, in public.

Having explicitly articulated values — shared by both partners — makes decisions in those moments faster and more consistent. It also surfaces misalignment before it becomes conflict.

---

## Questions to Discuss and Answer Together

### Core Values
- What 3–5 values do we most want to instill in our child?
- What does a successful childhood look like to us? What does a successful adult child look like?
- What character traits do we most want to nurture?

### Discipline
- What is our philosophy on discipline?
- What role does shame play (it shouldn't)?
- How do we balance accountability with grace?
- What were our childhoods like, and what do we want to replicate vs. change?

### Relationships and Family
- How important is extended family involvement?
- How do we handle disagreement in front of our child?
- How do we talk about our relationship to our child — modeling what love looks like?

### Worldview and Faith
- What role, if any, does religious or spiritual upbringing play?
- How do we want to talk about the world, other people, and social issues?

### Screens and Technology
- What is our philosophy on screens in the first years?
- How do we model healthy technology use?

### Independence and Risk
- How much independence and risk do we allow, and when?
- What is our tolerance for physical risk (climbing, outdoor play)?

## Revisiting This Note

Parenting values evolve. Revisit this annually — what mattered most with a newborn may shift with a toddler, then a school-aged child. The exercise is not to lock in answers but to keep the conversation active.
