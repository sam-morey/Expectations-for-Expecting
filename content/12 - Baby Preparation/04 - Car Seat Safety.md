# Car Seat Safety

The car seat is the single most important safety purchase for a new baby. Installation errors are extremely common — even among parents who believe they've done it correctly.

---

## Types of Car Seats

### Infant Car Seat (Rear-Facing Only)
- Designed for newborns to ~22–35 lbs (varies by seat)
- Rear-facing only; bucket handle for easy carry
- **Pros:** Easy to snap in and out; bucket carry; good for newborns
- **Cons:** Baby outgrows it by 9–18 months; shorter-term use

### Convertible Car Seat
- Starts rear-facing; converts to forward-facing when child meets weight/height criteria
- Stays installed in car; not portable
- **Pros:** Longer use (rear-facing to ~40–50 lbs, then forward-facing beyond); cost-effective long-term
- **Cons:** Less portable; no carry handle; filling both roles means selecting from first use

### All-in-One / 3-in-1
- Rear-facing → forward-facing → booster in one seat
- Maximum longevity
- **Cons:** Large; heavy; may not fit every vehicle

---

## For Newborns: Rear-Facing Is Non-Negotiable

**Always rear-facing until the child outgrows the seat's rear-facing height/weight limit** — not just until age 2.

The American Academy of Pediatrics updated its guidance: children should remain rear-facing as long as possible (until they exceed the seat's limits). For most children this is ages 3–5.

**Why rear-facing is safer:** In a frontal crash (most common), a rear-facing seat distributes crash forces across the back, head, and neck — the strongest parts of the body. Forward-facing seats use a harness but allow the head and neck to be pulled forward violently by crash momentum.

---

## Installation: The Basics

**The most common error:** Installing the seat too loosely. At the belt path, the seat should not move more than 1 inch side-to-side or front-to-back when tested with two hands.

**LATCH System (Lower Anchors and Tethers for Children):**
- Use LATCH or seat belt — not both (unless manufacturer instructions say otherwise)
- LATCH connectors have weight limits (~65 lbs combined child + seat weight; check manufacturer)
- Top tether used when forward-facing (always); some seats have top tethers for rear-facing too

**Recline Angle:**
- Newborns must be in the correct recline (most seats have indicators)
- Too upright: baby's head falls forward, airway compromised
- Check seat instructions for newborn angle requirements

---

## Have Your Installation Inspected

**Statistics:** ~46% of car seats are installed or used incorrectly.

**Free inspection:** Certified Child Passenger Safety Technicians (CPSTs) provide free inspections at many fire stations, hospitals, and Safe Kids coalitions.

Find an inspection station: safekids.org

**When to have it inspected:**
- When first installed
- After any car accident (seat must be replaced after moderate or severe crash)
- When converting from rear-facing to forward-facing

## The Car Seat Harness

**5-point harness must be:**
- Snug — you cannot pinch excess webbing at the shoulder
- Buckle at crotch level (not stomach)
- Chest clip at armpit level (heart clip — not belly)
- Shoulder straps at or below baby's shoulders (rear-facing)

**Coats and winter gear:** Bulky outerwear compresses in a crash, creating harness slack. Do not put baby in a car seat in a puffy coat. Use a car seat cover that goes over the harness, or warm baby with a blanket over the straps.

---

## Car Seat Expiration

Car seats expire — typically 6–10 years from manufacture date (stamped on the seat). Plastic degrades with UV and temperature cycling. Do not use expired seats or seats of unknown history.

**Never use a secondhand car seat** unless you know its full history — free from accidents, not expired, and with all original parts.
