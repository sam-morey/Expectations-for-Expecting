Safe sleep practices are the most evidence-backed intervention for reducing the risk of Sudden Infant Death Syndrome (SIDS) and sleep-related infant deaths. The guidelines are non-negotiable for every sleep, every time.

---

## The ABCs of Safe Sleep

**A — Alone**  
Baby should sleep alone — no adults, siblings, or pets in the sleep space.

**B — Back**  
Always place baby on their back to sleep, from birth. Every time.

**C — Crib (or Safe Sleep Surface)**  
Firm, flat sleep surface in an approved sleep environment.

---

## The Full Safe Sleep Environment

| Safe | Unsafe |
|---|---|
| Firm, flat mattress | Inclined sleeper, wedge, bouncer |
| Fitted sheet only | Loose blankets, pillows |
| Empty crib | Bumper pads (even "breathable" ones) |
| Back position | Stomach or side position |
| Room temperature 68–72°F | Overheating |
| Own sleep space | Adult bed (co-sleeping) |
| Room-sharing (same room, separate surface) | Sofa, armchair, or car seat (for sleep outside car) |

---

## Why Back is Best

Before the "Back to Sleep" campaign launched in 1994, SIDS rates in the US were ~1.2 per 1,000 live births. They dropped over 50% within a decade of the recommendation. The evidence is clear.

**Parents often worry about back sleeping:**
- *"What if they roll to their stomach?"* — Once baby can roll independently both ways (~4–6 months), it's safe to leave them in whatever position they roll to during sleep. You still place them on their back to start.
- *"What about the flat head?"* — Positional plagiocephaly is manageable with tummy time while awake and supervised. SIDS is not.

---

## Inclined Sleepers: Do Not Use

The Fisher-Price Rock 'n Play and many similar products were recalled after dozens of infant deaths. The Consumer Product Safety Commission now requires that infant sleep products have an incline ≤10 degrees.

Products marketed as "soothers," "nappers," or "rockers" are not safe sleep surfaces. If baby falls asleep in any inclined device, transfer to a flat surface.

---

## Room-Sharing vs. Bed-Sharing

**Room-sharing is recommended.** Sleeping in the same room as baby (but on separate surfaces) for 6–12 months reduces SIDS risk by up to 50%.

**Bed-sharing increases SIDS risk**, especially with:
- Infants under 4 months
- Premature or low birth weight babies
- Parental smoking (even outside)
- Parental alcohol or medication use
- Soft mattresses

**If you fall asleep while feeding:** On a firm, flat surface with baby away from pillows and blankets, this carries less risk than a sofa or armchair. It is still not recommended, but if it happens, move baby to their sleep space as soon as you wake.

---

## Additional Safe Sleep Practices

- **Offer a pacifier at sleep time** — associated with reduced SIDS risk (if breastfeeding, wait until feeding is established, ~3–4 weeks)
- **Keep room cool** — 68–72°F; overheating is a risk factor
- **No smoking** — Parental smoking is one of the strongest SIDS risk factors
- **Tummy time while awake** — Builds strength; prevents flat head; do not substitute for back sleeping

---

## When Safe Sleep Guidelines Change

- At ~4–6 months, when baby can roll both directions independently: you still place on back, but don't reposition if they roll during sleep
- The firm, flat surface requirement remains throughout infancy
