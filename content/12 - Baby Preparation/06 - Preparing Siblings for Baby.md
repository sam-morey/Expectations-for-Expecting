Helping an older child transition to being a "big sibling" is a delicate balance of excitement and mourning for their time as the center of your world. Early involvement and realistic expectations are key.

---

## When and How to Tell Them

| Age | When to Tell | How to Explain |
|---|---|---|
| **Toddler (under 3)** | When you start showing or when they ask. | Keep it concrete: "A baby is growing in Mommy's belly." Use picture books. |
| **Preschool (3–5)** | After the first trimester or anatomy scan. | Focus on what *they* can do (help with diapers, sing to baby). Prepare for "waiting" time. |
| **School Age (6+)** | As soon as you feel comfortable. | Explain the science and the reality: babies cry, sleep, and eat a lot. |

---

## Preparation During Pregnancy

- **The "Big" Transition:** Move the older child to a "big kid bed" or change their room at least 2–3 months before the baby arrives, so they don't feel "pushed out" by the newborn.
- **Baby Dolls:** For younger kids, practicing "gentle hands" with a doll can be helpful.
- **Visit the Hospital:** If possible, show them where the baby will be born so they can visualize it.
- **Read Together:** There are many excellent books about becoming a big sibling (e.g., *The New Baby* by Mercer Mayer or *I'm a Big Sister/Brother* by Joanna Cole).

---

## The First Meeting

- **Hands-Free Greeting:** When the sibling first walks into the hospital room or home, try to have the baby in the bassinet rather than in your arms. This allows you to give the older child a big hug first.
- **A Gift from the Baby:** Many parents find success with the "baby" bringing a gift for the older sibling.
- **The "Big Sibling" Role:** Give them a specific, age-appropriate job (e.g., "Chief Diaper Fetcher" or "Song Leader").

---

## Managing the Shift

- **One-on-One Time:** Protect at least 15 minutes a day of "Special Time" with the older child where the baby is not present.
- **Expect Regression:** It is very common for older siblings to regress in potty training, sleep, or behavior. This is a sign they need extra connection, not more discipline.
- **Acknowledge the Hard Parts:** It's okay to say, "It's hard to wait for Mommy to finish nursing. I can't wait to play with you in 5 minutes."
