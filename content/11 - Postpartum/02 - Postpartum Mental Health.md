# Postpartum Mental Health

Postpartum mental health conditions are common, treatable, and often undiagnosed. They are caused by a combination of hormonal upheaval, sleep deprivation, physical recovery, and identity transition — not personal weakness.

---

## The Spectrum

| Condition | Prevalence | Onset | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| Baby blues | 50–80% | Days 3–5 | Resolves by week 2 |
| Postpartum depression (PPD) | 10–15% | Any time in first year | Months if untreated |
| Postpartum anxiety (PPA) | 10–15% | Any time in first year | Months if untreated |
| Postpartum OCD | 3–5% | Often first weeks | Variable |
| Postpartum PTSD | 3–9% | After traumatic birth experience | Variable |
| Postpartum psychosis | 0.1–0.2% | Days to weeks; often rapid | Medical emergency |

---

## Baby Blues

Normal; affects most new mothers.

**Symptoms:** Tearfulness, mood swings, feeling overwhelmed, irritability, anxiety — appearing in the first week after birth

**Cause:** The most dramatic hormonal drop in human physiology — estrogen and progesterone fall from pregnancy peak to near zero within 24–72 hours of delivery

**Timeline:** Begins days 3–5; resolves by 2 weeks

**Not PPD:** Baby blues are time-limited and don't require treatment beyond support, rest, and validation.

If symptoms persist beyond 2 weeks or are severe at any point → screen for PPD.

---

## Postpartum Depression (PPD)

Not a character flaw. Not caused by not loving your baby. A medical condition driven by neurobiological changes.

**Symptoms (persistent, >2 weeks):**
- Persistent sadness, emptiness, or hopelessness
- Loss of interest or pleasure in activities
- Difficulty bonding with baby (guilt-inducing but common in PPD)
- Feeling like a bad mother; excessive self-criticism
- Crying frequently or not being able to cry when you want to
- Difficulty functioning; withdrawing from support
- Intrusive thoughts about harming self or baby (seek help immediately)

**Risk factors:**
- History of depression or anxiety
- PPD in a previous pregnancy
- Poor social support
- Significant life stressors
- Birth trauma
- Premature birth or NICU admission

**Treatment:** Therapy (CBT; interpersonal therapy), antidepressants (safe while breastfeeding — sertraline, paroxetine), or both. Highly effective. Most women improve significantly within weeks of starting treatment.

---

## Postpartum Anxiety (PPA)

Often underdiagnosed because anxiety symptoms are seen as "normal" for new parents.

**Symptoms:**
- Constant worry about baby's health and safety
- Inability to sleep even when the baby sleeps
- Physical symptoms: racing heart, shallow breathing, dizziness
- Intrusive "what if" thoughts
- Overprotective behavior that interferes with functioning
- Panic attacks

**Treatment:** Same as PPD; therapy and/or medication. Very treatable.

---

## Postpartum Psychosis

Rare but a medical emergency.

**Symptoms:** Hallucinations, delusions, confusion, rapid mood changes, paranoia, inability to sleep for days, bizarre behavior

**Onset:** Usually within the first 2 weeks; can appear very rapidly (within 48 hours)

**Action:** Emergency medical attention immediately. This is not PPD — it is a psychiatric emergency.

**Risk factor:** Bipolar disorder significantly increases risk. If you or a family member has bipolar disorder, discuss postpartum psychosis prevention with your provider during pregnancy.

---

## Screening

ACOG recommends universal screening for perinatal depression and anxiety. The **Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS)** is commonly used at the 6-week postpartum visit.

**Don't wait for screening.** If you feel something is wrong, call your provider.

---

## Getting Help

**In the US:**
- Postpartum Support International: postpartum.net (helpline: 1-800-944-4773)
- SAMHSA National Helpline: 1-800-662-4357
- Ask your OB, midwife, or pediatrician at any appointment
