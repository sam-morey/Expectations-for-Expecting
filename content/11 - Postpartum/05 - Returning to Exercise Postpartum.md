Returning to exercise postpartum requires more patience than most fitness-oriented people expect. The body underwent 9+ months of structural change; returning safely takes weeks to months, not days.

---

## Why the Timeline Matters

Postpartum recovery involves:
- Healing of the uterus, placenta site, and any tears or incision
- **Pelvic floor rehabilitation** — stretched and potentially damaged from pregnancy and birth
- **Diastasis recti recovery** — separation of the rectus abdominis muscles
- Ligament laxity from relaxin (persists for months, longer with breastfeeding)
- Sleep deprivation and caloric demands of breastfeeding

Returning to intense exercise before these systems are ready increases risk of pelvic floor dysfunction, prolapse, and injury.

---

## The General Timeline

### Weeks 1–2: Rest and Healing
- Walking short distances (around the house → block → neighborhood)
- Gentle pelvic floor exercises (Kegels) if comfortable — even day 1
- Nothing more; the body is in acute healing mode

### Weeks 2–6: Progressive Walking
- Gradually increase walking distance and pace
- Continue pelvic floor work
- Diastasis recti check (see below)
- No running, heavy lifting, HIIT, or core-loading exercises

### 6-Week Appointment
- Standard provider clearance — but this clears for general activity, not high-impact exercise
- Many providers give blanket clearance without pelvic floor assessment
- **Consider seeing a pelvic floor PT** at 6–8 weeks for individualized clearance

### Weeks 6–12: Graduated Return (Vaginal Birth)
- Begin strength training at light loads; focus on form and breathing
- Begin jogging (short intervals) only if:
  - No leaking (urine/feces)
  - No pelvic heaviness/pressure during or after exercise
  - No pain
- Follow a progressive return-to-running program (e.g., Groom & Moore guidelines)

### Weeks 8–12+: Cesarean Recovery
- Additional 2–4 weeks of minimal core loading vs. vaginal birth
- Scar tissue mobilization (after wound fully closed, ~week 6)
- Running/jumping/HIIT: typically week 12+ with pelvic floor PT clearance

## Diastasis Recti Assessment

Diastasis recti (DR) is a gap in the linea alba — the connective tissue between the two sides of the rectus abdominis. Some separation is normal and expected; problematic diastasis requires rehabilitation.

**Self-check:**
1. Lie on your back, knees bent
2. Place 2–3 fingers horizontally at the navel
3. Lift your head slightly (mini-crunch)
4. Feel for a gap between the muscle bellies; measure width and depth

**Normal:** Gap <2 fingers wide that feels firm with tension  
**Significant:** Gap >2–3 fingers, or gap that feels soft and doesn't tension up with head lift

**Treatment:** Pelvic floor PT; specific rehabilitation exercises; avoid loading that causes the midline to dome or cone until healed.

---

## Warning Signs: Slow Down or Stop

These symptoms during or after postpartum exercise indicate the body isn't ready:
- Urinary or fecal leakage
- Pelvic heaviness or pressure (prolapse symptom)
- Pain (pelvic, hip, abdominal, incision)
- Increased lochia (bleeding) after exercise
- Coning or doming in the abdominal midline during exercise

These are feedback signals — not failures. Reduce load or intensity; see a pelvic floor PT.

---

## Realistic Expectations

Most fit people want to "get their body back" quickly. The more realistic framing:
- At 6 weeks, you are 15% recovered from birth
- At 3 months, you are significantly better
- At 6–12 months, most people feel close to their pre-pregnancy baseline
- Breastfeeding maintains relaxin and ligament laxity; injury risk remains elevated while nursing

Be patient with the timeline. The goal is returning to full function — not rushing back and creating injuries that set recovery back further.
