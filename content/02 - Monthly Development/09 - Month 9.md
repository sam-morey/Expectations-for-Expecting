---

The final month. Baby is fully developed and waiting. The cervix begins ripening, contractions may start and stop, and every day closer to the due date — and beyond — is baby continuing to prepare.

---

## Baby This Month

- Week 36: Head often engaging into the pelvis ("lightening"); shedding lanugo and vernix; lungs mature; brain and nervous system still developing (will continue for years after birth)
- Week 37: "Full term" — all major systems ready; baby is considered early term at 37–38 weeks
- Week 38: Lungs, brain, and liver reach full maturity; losing vernix; fingernails long enough to scratch
- Week 39: "Full term" — optimal brain and organ maturity; birth most common this week
- Week 40: Due date; average weight 7–8 lbs; 19–21 inches; ready to meet the world

**If pregnancy continues past 40 weeks:**
- 41 weeks: Post-dates; more frequent monitoring begins
- 42 weeks: Induction typically offered or recommended; placental function monitoring important

---

## Mom This Month

**Common symptoms:**
- **Lightening** — Baby drops; easier to breathe; more pelvic pressure; more frequent urination
- **Increased Braxton Hicks** — Cervix ripening; prelabor can feel like many false starts
- **Mucus plug** — Expelled as cervix opens; can be days to weeks before labor; may be tinged with blood ("bloody show")
- **Nesting peak** — Strongest urge to clean, organize, and prepare
- **Loose stools** — Common the day before labor onset; prostaglandins clearing the bowel
- **Insomnia** — Anxiety, discomfort, and frequent waking peak
- **Contractions** — Braxton Hicks increasing; prodromal labor (irregular but real contractions) common

---

## Signs of True Labor

- Contractions become regular, progressive (closer together, longer, stronger)
- Contractions continue despite rest, walking, or position change
- Time contractions: **5-1-1 rule** — contractions 5 minutes apart, lasting 1 minute, for 1 hour → call provider
- Water breaking (may be a gush or a trickle; call provider)
- Bloody show + progressive contractions

See [[02 - Signs of Labor]] for full detail.

---

## Medical This Month

- Appointments weekly (36–40 weeks)
- Cervical checks (dilation and effacement) — provider-dependent; not predictive of labor onset
- Non-stress test (NST) if post-dates or indicated
- Induction conversation at 39–41 weeks based on clinical situation
- GBS test results in chart; antibiotics ordered if positive

---

## The "Waiting" Phase

The final weeks before labor often involve significant emotional variability — anticipation, anxiety, physical misery, and joy all at once. This is normal. Some strategies:

- Stay occupied with manageable activities
- Avoid checking in with many people who will ask "any news?"
- Rest as much as possible — labor is physically demanding
- Trust the process; the body knows what to do

---

## What to Do This Month

- [ ] All preparation complete (hospital bag, car seat, nursery)
- [ ] Know the signs of labor and when to call the provider
- [ ] Know the route to the hospital; confirm parking and check-in process
- [ ] Confirm on-call coverage with doula
- [ ] Plan who to call / notify when labor begins
- [ ] Have phone charged; camera ready
- [ ] Rest whenever possible
