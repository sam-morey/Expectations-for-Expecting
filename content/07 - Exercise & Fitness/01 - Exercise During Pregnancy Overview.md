# Exercise During Pregnancy Overview

Exercise during pregnancy is safe, beneficial, and recommended for most women. The goal shifts from performance or fat loss to health maintenance, managing symptoms, preparing for labor, and supporting postpartum recovery.

---

## Benefits of Exercise During Pregnancy

| Benefit | Evidence |
|---|---|
| Reduces gestational diabetes risk | ~30–50% risk reduction with regular moderate exercise |
| Reduces preeclampsia risk | Associated with lower incidence |
| Controls gestational weight gain | Significant effect on staying within guidelines |
| Reduces back pain | Core and postural strength; one of the most evidence-backed pregnancy exercise benefits |
| Improves sleep quality | Better sleep onset and quality |
| Reduces anxiety and depression | Significant effect; exercise is as effective as therapy for mild-moderate depression |
| Shorter labor duration | Some evidence (though effect is modest) |
| Better fetal outcomes | Babies of active mothers show improved cardiovascular function |
| Faster postpartum recovery | Maintained fitness base returns more quickly |

---

## Safety Guidelines

### ACOG (American College of OB/GYN) Recommendations
- **Minimum:** 150 minutes/week of moderate-intensity exercise
- **Intensity:** Should be able to speak a sentence (the "talk test"); rate of perceived exertion 5–6/10
- Heart rate targets are no longer the primary guide — perceived exertion is more practical
- Continue pre-pregnancy fitness activities, adapted as needed

### Absolute Contraindications (Do Not Exercise Without Clearance)
- Hemodynamically significant heart disease
- Restrictive lung disease
- Incompetent cervix or cerclage
- Placenta previa after 26 weeks
- Preterm labor (current episode)
- Severe preeclampsia
- Multiple gestation (twins) at risk for preterm labor

### Relative Contraindications (Discuss with Provider)
- Preterm labor history
- Anemia
- Poorly controlled type 1 diabetes, hypertension, or thyroid disease
- Extreme obesity or extreme underweight

---

## What to Avoid

| Avoid | Why |
|---|---|
| Contact sports | Impact and fall risk |
| Scuba diving | Decompression sickness risk to fetus |
| Skydiving, high-altitude exercise (>8,000 ft, if unacclimatized) | Altitude and oxygen risk |
| Hot yoga / sauna / hot tub | Core temperature elevation >102°F (39°C) associated with neural tube defects in first trimester |
| Supine positions after ~20 weeks (prolonged) | Inferior vena cava compression reduces blood return to heart |
| Valsalva maneuver (extreme breath-holding during heavy lifting) | Pressure spike; modify as needed |
| High fall risk activities | Rock climbing, skiing, mountain biking |

## Exercise by Trimester

### First Trimester
- **Continue prior exercise with minimal modification**
- Nausea and fatigue may reduce exercise capacity — this is normal; do what you can
- Avoid overheating (especially weeks 4–8 during neural tube formation)
- Stay well hydrated

### Second Trimester
- The most comfortable trimester for most women
- **Modify supine exercises** by second trimester — avoid flat-on-back positions for >3 minutes (use prop under right hip)
- Center of gravity shifts → reduce high-balance-required activities
- Round ligament pain may interrupt workouts; warm up slowly

### Third Trimester
- Reduce intensity as needed; listen to your body
- Shifting center of gravity increases fall risk — modify accordingly
- Walking, swimming, prenatal yoga, modified strength training all appropriate
- Pelvic floor work becomes more important
- Birth preparation exercises (squats, perineal massage prep, breathing practice)

---

## Stop Exercising and Contact Your Provider If:
- Vaginal bleeding
- Dyspnea (severe shortness of breath before exertion)
- Dizziness or faintness
- Regular contractions (before 37 weeks especially)
- Calf pain or swelling (possible DVT)
- Leaking fluid
- Chest pain
- Decreased fetal movement
