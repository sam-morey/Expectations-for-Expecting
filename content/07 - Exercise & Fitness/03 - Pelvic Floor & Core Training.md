# Pelvic Floor & Core Training

The pelvic floor is one of the most important areas to train during pregnancy — and one of the most neglected. A strong, functional pelvic floor supports the growing uterus, reduces incontinence, aids in labor and delivery, and accelerates postpartum recovery.

---

## What Is the Pelvic Floor?

The pelvic floor is a group of muscles and connective tissue that form a hammock at the base of the pelvis, supporting the bladder, uterus, and rectum. During pregnancy, these muscles bear increasing weight as the uterus grows.

**Functions:**
- Supports pelvic organs against gravity and intra-abdominal pressure
- Controls urinary and fecal continence
- Participates in sexual function
- Plays an active role in the second stage of labor (pushing and stretching)

---

## Why Pelvic Floor Work Matters in Pregnancy

- Urinary incontinence (leaking when coughing, sneezing, laughing) affects ~40% of pregnant women — it is common but not inevitable
- A functional pelvic floor (not just strong) helps with birth: the ability to relax and lengthen during pushing is as important as the ability to contract
- Pelvic floor dysfunction is easier to prevent than treat postpartum

---

## Kegel Exercises

Kegels contract and strengthen the pelvic floor muscles.

### How to Find the Right Muscles
Imagine stopping the flow of urine midstream — the muscles you squeeze are the pelvic floor. Do not actually do this while urinating (it can disrupt normal bladder emptying).

### Basic Kegel Protocol
1. Contract pelvic floor muscles
2. Hold for 5–10 seconds
3. Fully release (the release is equally important)
4. Repeat 10–15 times
5. 3 sets per day

**Progress:** Increase hold time and number of repetitions as strength improves.

**Common mistake:** Holding the breath or contracting the glutes and thighs instead. Only the pelvic floor should contract.

---

## Pelvic Floor Relaxation

Relaxation is as important as strength — especially for birth. A pelvic floor that cannot fully lengthen during the second stage of labor increases the risk of tearing.

**Practice:**
- Diaphragmatic breathing: As you inhale deeply, the pelvic floor naturally descends and relaxes. Focus on feeling this
- "Happy baby" pose, deep squats, and child's pose encourage pelvic floor lengthening
- Visualization: Imagine a flower opening or a hammock releasing — some women find imagery helpful

---

## Core Training in Pregnancy

**The goal changes from "core strength" to "core management"** — managing intra-abdominal pressure to protect the linea alba (connective tissue running down the abdomen) from diastasis recti.

### What to Avoid
- Traditional crunches and sit-ups after first trimester — high intra-abdominal pressure loads the linea alba
- Double leg lowers
- Heavy loaded spinal flexion
- Exercises where you see your belly "coning" or "doming" — this indicates excess pressure on the midline

### What to Do Instead

| Exercise | What It Trains |
|---|---|
| Diaphragmatic breathing | Core pressure management; pelvic floor coordination |
| Bird-dog | Anti-rotation stability; back and deep core |
| Dead bug (modified) | Core stability; no spinal flexion |
| Side plank | Lateral stability; no anterior core compression |
| Pallof press | Anti-rotation; functional core |
| Heel slides | Deep core activation; appropriate loading |
| Glute bridges | Glutes + posterior pelvic floor; safe in all trimesters (modify to incline late pregnancy) |
| Squats | Hip and glute strength; birth preparation |

---

## Seeing a Pelvic Floor Physical Therapist

A pelvic floor PT (physiotherapist) specializes in the pelvic floor and is one of the highest-ROI prenatal investments available.

**When to see one:**
- Any time during pregnancy (proactively, not just when problems arise)
- Urinary leakage
- Pelvic pain (SPD, round ligament, tailbone)
- First pregnancy (baseline assessment)
- Postpartum recovery

A pelvic floor PT assesses the specific function of your pelvic floor — whether it's too tight (hypertonic), too weak, or has coordination issues — and provides a personalized program. Kegels are not appropriate for everyone; a hypertonic pelvic floor needs relaxation work, not more squeezing.
