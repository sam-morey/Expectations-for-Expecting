A prenatal vitamin fills nutritional gaps — it does not replace a healthy diet, but provides critical insurance for nutrients that are difficult to get sufficiently through food alone during pregnancy.

---

## When to Start

**Ideally before conception.** Folic acid must be present during weeks 3–4 when the neural tube closes — before most women know they're pregnant. If planning pregnancy, start a prenatal vitamin 1–3 months before trying.

If the pregnancy is unplanned, start as soon as the pregnancy is confirmed.

---

## What to Look for in a Prenatal Vitamin

| Nutrient | Amount to Look For | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Folate (folic acid or methylfolate) | 400–800mcg | Neural tube closure (weeks 3–4) |
| Iron | 27mg | Supports increased blood volume; fetal iron stores |
| Calcium | 150–200mg | Bone development (most prenatal vitamins have less than the daily requirement — get the rest from food) |
| Vitamin D | 600–1,000 IU | Bone health; immune function; deficiency common |
| DHA (omega-3) | 200–300mg | Brain and eye development |
| Iodine | 150mcg | Thyroid function; fetal brain development |
| Choline | 250–450mg | Brain development (most vitamins have little; supplement separately if needed) |
| Vitamin B12 | 2.6mcg+ | Nervous system; critical for vegans and vegetarians |
| Zinc | 11mg | Cell growth; immune function |
| Vitamin C | 85mg+ | Iron absorption; tissue formation |

**Methylfolate vs. folic acid:** Women with MTHFR gene variants may have reduced ability to convert folic acid to its active form. Methylfolate (5-MTHF) bypasses this conversion. Ask your provider if you've been tested.

---

## Supplement Priorities Beyond the Prenatal Vitamin

### DHA / Omega-3
Most prenatal vitamins contain little or no DHA. If yours doesn't, supplement separately.
- **Dose:** 200–300mg DHA/day
- **Sources:** Fish oil capsules; algae-based (vegetarian/vegan)

### Vitamin D
Many women are deficient. Prenatal vitamins often include 400 IU, but 1,000–2,000 IU/day may be appropriate if blood levels are low.
- Ask for vitamin D to be tested at your first appointment

### Choline
Critically underconsumed and underrepresented in prenatal vitamins.
- **Target:** 450mg/day total from food + supplements
- **Best food sources:** Eggs (2 eggs = ~250mg choline)
- If you don't eat eggs, consider a separate choline supplement

### Magnesium
Not required but beneficial: helps with sleep, leg cramps, constipation, and headaches. 200–350mg/day as magnesium glycinate is well-tolerated.

### Probiotics
Not required but safe and possibly beneficial for reducing gestational diabetes risk and supporting infant gut microbiome at birth.

---

## Common Supplement Forms and Tolerability

| Form | Notes |
|---|---|
| Gummy prenatal vitamins | Often lack iron; check the label; some women tolerate better |
| Capsule | Most common; easier to swallow than large tablets |
| Softgel | Good if DHA included; easy to swallow |
| Powder / liquid | Option for severe nausea when pills are difficult |

**Iron and nausea:** Iron in prenatal vitamins causes nausea and constipation in some women. Taking the vitamin at night with food helps. If severe, ask provider about a different iron form (ferrous bisglycinate is gentler than ferrous sulfate).

---

## Supplements to Avoid (Not Recommended in Pregnancy)

| Supplement | Concern |
|---|---|
| High-dose vitamin A (retinol form) | Teratogenic at high doses (>10,000 IU/day); beta-carotene form is safe |
| High-dose vitamin E (>800 IU/day) | Associated with adverse outcomes |
| Herbal supplements (most) | Unregulated; many untested for pregnancy safety |
| Weight loss supplements | Unsafe without exception |
| Detox or cleanse products | No role in pregnancy |
