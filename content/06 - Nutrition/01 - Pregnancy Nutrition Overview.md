# Pregnancy Nutrition Overview

Pregnancy nutrition is not about eating for two — it's about eating better for one, then gradually increasing calories as the pregnancy progresses. Most of what a pregnant woman eats every day matters more for the baby's development than any supplement.

---

## Caloric Needs by Trimester

| Trimester | Additional Calories Needed | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| First (weeks 1–13) | ~0–100 extra kcal/day | Embryo is tiny; no significant caloric increase needed |
| Second (weeks 14–27) | ~340 extra kcal/day | Growth accelerates; energy demands increase |
| Third (weeks 28–40) | ~450 extra kcal/day | Rapid fetal weight gain; highest energy demand |

**"Eating for two" is a myth** — most women only need a small, gradual caloric increase.

## Macronutrient Priorities

### Protein
- **Requirement:** ~70–100g/day (RDA is 71g; higher intake supports optimal fetal development and maternal tissue)
- **Critical for:** Fetal cell growth, placenta development, increased maternal blood volume, breast tissue
- **Sources:** Chicken, fish, eggs, Greek yogurt, legumes, tofu, dairy

### Folate / Folic Acid
- **Critical period:** Weeks 3–4 (neural tube closure) — before most women know they're pregnant
- **Requirement:** 400–800mcg daily; 4mg for high-risk pregnancies (prior neural tube defect, certain medications, diabetes)
- **Food sources:** Dark leafy greens, legumes, fortified cereals, citrus
- **Supplement form:** Folic acid (synthetic) or methylfolate (more bioavailable; required for MTHFR variants)

### Iron
- **Requirement:** 27mg/day (nearly double the non-pregnant requirement)
- **Why:** Blood volume increases 40–50%; fetal iron stores building for first 6 months of life
- **Sources:** Red meat (most bioavailable), lentils, fortified cereals, spinach
- **Absorption tip:** Pair plant-based iron with vitamin C; avoid calcium at same meal
- **Signs of deficiency:** Fatigue, pallor, shortness of breath; CBC screens at first appointment

### Calcium
- **Requirement:** 1,000mg/day
- **Why:** Fetal bone and teeth formation; if maternal intake insufficient, fetal needs pull from maternal bone stores
- **Sources:** Dairy, fortified plant milk, sardines with bones, tofu, broccoli, almonds

### Omega-3 Fatty Acids (DHA/EPA)
- **Requirement:** 200–300mg DHA/day
- **Critical for:** Fetal brain and eye development (DHA constitutes 15–20% of brain cortex)
- **Sources:** Fatty fish (salmon, sardines, trout), algae-based supplements (for vegetarians)
- **Avoid:** High-mercury fish (see [[02 - Foods to Avoid]])

### Choline
- **Requirement:** 450mg/day (most prenatal vitamins provide <50mg)
- **Critical for:** Brain development, neural tube closure, memory function
- **Sources:** Eggs (yolks especially), meat, fish, legumes
- **Note:** Highly underconsumed during pregnancy; eat eggs

## Hydration

- **Target:** 8–10 cups (64–80 oz) of fluids daily; more in heat or with heavy exercise
- Dehydration worsens nausea, headaches, constipation, and Braxton Hicks
- Water, sparkling water, herbal teas (pregnancy-safe), milk all count
- **Caffeine:** Limit to ≤200mg/day (approximately 1 medium coffee)

## Eating Well with Nausea

First trimester nausea makes ideal nutrition difficult. Priorities:
1. Keep prenatal vitamin down (even if that's all that happens)
2. Eat whatever is tolerable — plain carbohydrates are fine in the first trimester
3. Focus on protein when possible (most filling, most important)
4. Add vegetables and fruits when nausea resolves

**Do not stress about first trimester nutrition** — the embryo is tiny and draws from maternal stores. The prenatal vitamin fills critical gaps.
