# When to Call Your Doctor

Knowing what's a normal symptom vs. a warning sign is one of the most important things to understand during pregnancy. When in doubt, call — providers would rather take a reassurance call than have a serious complication go unreported.

---

## The Rule

**If you are unsure whether something is an emergency, call.** You will never be judged for checking in. Being wrong about a non-emergency is far better than waiting on an emergency.

---

## Call Immediately / Go to the ER or L&D

These symptoms require immediate evaluation — do not wait for a callback:

| Symptom | Why |
|---|---|
| Heavy vaginal bleeding | May indicate placental abruption, previa, or miscarriage |
| Signs of preeclampsia: severe headache + vision changes + upper right abdominal pain + sudden swelling | Life-threatening blood pressure disorder |
| Water breaking (fluid leaking from vagina) | Rupture of membranes; risk of infection; labor may begin |
| No fetal movement after 24 weeks — baby not moving | Fetal wellbeing assessment needed urgently |
| Regular contractions before 37 weeks | Possible preterm labor |
| Chest pain or difficulty breathing | Rare but can indicate pulmonary embolism (DVT risk elevated in pregnancy) |
| Sudden severe abdominal pain | Multiple possible causes requiring immediate evaluation |
| Feeling faint or losing consciousness | |
| High fever (>101°F) | |
| Signs of stroke (facial drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty) | |

---

## Call Your Provider Same Day

| Symptom | Why |
|---|---|
| Spotting or light bleeding at any stage | May be normal, may need evaluation |
| Fever 100.4–101°F | May indicate infection |
| Burning with urination | UTIs are common and dangerous in pregnancy; treat promptly |
| Severe nausea/vomiting — unable to keep liquids down | Dehydration risk; may need medication or IV fluids |
| Significant decrease in fetal movement (after 24 weeks) | Trust your instincts; movement patterns matter |
| Headache not responding to Tylenol | Screen for preeclampsia |
| Significant new swelling in face or hands | Preeclampsia sign |
| Itching on palms and soles of feet | Screen for cholestasis of pregnancy |
| Vaginal discharge that's unusual color, odor, or texture | May indicate infection |
| Rash, especially with fever | Various causes; some dangerous in pregnancy |
| Suspected broken bone or trauma | Car accidents — even minor ones; always get evaluated in pregnancy |

---

## When to Call About Contractions

| Situation | Action |
|---|---|
| Before 37 weeks: 4+ regular contractions per hour | Call immediately — possible preterm labor |
| 37+ weeks: 5-1-1 rule — contractions 5 min apart, lasting 1 minute, for 1 hour | Call your provider / head to L&D |
| Water broken + any contractions | Go to L&D |
| Contractions but irregular and stopping with rest/hydration | Braxton Hicks — call if not sure |

---

## Fetal Movement: Trust Your Gut

You know your baby's movement patterns better than any chart. If something feels different — less movement than your normal — contact your provider. A non-stress test (NST) can confirm fetal wellbeing and takes 20–30 minutes.

**Do not Google your way to reassurance. Call your provider.**

---

## Medication Safety Reference

**Safe in pregnancy (with provider guidance):** Tylenol (acetaminophen), Tums, Pepcid, Benadryl, Claritin, saline nasal spray  
**Avoid without provider approval:** Ibuprofen (NSAIDs), aspirin (in standard doses), decongestants (especially in first trimester)  
**Ask before taking anything new** — including herbal supplements, which are not regulated or uniformly safe
