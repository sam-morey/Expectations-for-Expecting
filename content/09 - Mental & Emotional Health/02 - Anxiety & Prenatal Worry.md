---

Some anxiety about pregnancy, birth, and parenthood is normal. Persistent, intrusive, or disabling anxiety is a medical condition that deserves treatment — not dismissal.

---

## What's Normal

- Worrying about miscarriage, especially in the first trimester
- Anxiety before prenatal appointments ("white coat" anxiety)
- Fear of labor pain and birth complications
- Worry about whether you'll be a good parent
- Intrusive "what if" thoughts about rare bad outcomes
- Heightened emotional reactivity to news stories about pregnancy

**These are normal responses to a high-stakes life event.** They don't require treatment — they require acknowledgment, communication, and appropriate information.

---

## When Worry Becomes Anxiety Disorder

Anxiety becomes a clinical problem when it:
- Is persistent (most days for >2 weeks)
- Is difficult to control even with reassurance
- Interferes with daily functioning (sleep, work, relationships)
- Involves panic attacks (heart racing, shortness of breath, feeling of doom)
- Prevents engagement with prenatal care (avoiding appointments out of fear of bad news)
- Is intrusive and time-consuming

**Perinatal anxiety disorders** are more common than perinatal depression. They are undertreated because they're seen as "just worry."

---

## Evidence-Based Approaches

### Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)
Most effective non-medication approach for perinatal anxiety. Helps identify and reframe distorted thought patterns. Can be done in-person or via telehealth.

### Mindfulness-Based Approaches
Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) and similar programs have good evidence for prenatal anxiety. Not about eliminating anxious thoughts — about changing your relationship with them.

### Exercise
150 minutes/week of moderate exercise has effects comparable to medication for mild-moderate anxiety and depression. Walking counts.

### Medication
- SSRIs (most commonly sertraline/Zoloft) are used in pregnancy and are considered the best-studied, lowest-risk medication option
- The risk of untreated anxiety to the pregnancy (cortisol, sleep disruption, reduced prenatal care engagement) must be weighed against medication risk
- Do not stop psychiatric medication abruptly without discussing with your provider

## Managing Day-to-Day Anxiety

| Strategy | How It Helps |
|---|---|
| Limit Dr. Google | Symptom searches amplify anxiety; go to your provider instead |
| Have a "worry window" | Designate 20 minutes/day to worry; redirect at other times |
| Name the worst case, then the likely case | Externalizing fear reduces its power |
| Talk to your partner | Anxiety shared is anxiety halved; partner support reduces symptoms |
| Physical grounding | Deep breathing, cold water on face, grounding exercises interrupt the panic response |
| Reduce news and social media | Constant information input feeds anxiety |
| Ask your provider directly | Often, a question answered by your provider is worth more than an hour of Googling |

---

## The "Sunshine" Factor

A major driver of prenatal anxiety is the suppression of normal fear in favor of performing happiness. Culture tells pregnant women they should feel only joy — which means many feel shame when they feel fear, ambivalence, or dread.

**Naming your anxiety out loud — to a partner, friend, therapist, or provider — is the most important first step.** Anxiety hidden and unexpressed is anxiety amplified.
