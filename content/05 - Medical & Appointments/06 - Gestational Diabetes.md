Gestational Diabetes (GD) is a form of high blood sugar that affects pregnant women who didn't have diabetes before they were pregnant. It occurs when the placenta produces hormones that cause insulin resistance. It is manageable and usually resolves after birth.

---

## Screening and Diagnosis

- **The Glucose Challenge Test (GCT):** Usually performed between 24–28 weeks. You drink a sugary solution and have your blood drawn one hour later.
- **The Glucose Tolerance Test (GTT):** If the GCT is positive, you take a longer (3-hour) fasting test to confirm a diagnosis.

---

## Management

### Diet and Nutrition
The goal is to keep blood sugar stable.
- **Complex Carbs:** Focus on whole grains, beans, and vegetables.
- **Protein + Fiber:** Always pair carbohydrates with protein and fiber to slow glucose absorption.
- **Small, Frequent Meals:** 3 small meals and 2–3 snacks are usually better than 3 large meals.

### Monitoring and Exercise
- **Glucose Testing:** Most women with GD will need to prick their finger 4 times a day (fasting and 1–2 hours after each meal).
- **Exercise:** Even a 10–15 minute walk after meals can significantly lower blood sugar.

---

## Risks and Monitoring

If unmanaged, GD can lead to:
- **Macrosomia:** A baby that is significantly larger than average, which can complicate delivery.
- **Hypoglycemia:** Low blood sugar in the baby immediately after birth.
- **Preeclampsia Risk:** GD increases the risk of developing high blood pressure.

**Extra Monitoring:** You may have more frequent ultrasounds (growth scans) and Non-Stress Tests (NSTs) in the third trimester.

---

## Postpartum Follow-up

GD usually disappears immediately after the placenta is delivered. However:
- You will likely have a follow-up glucose test 6–12 weeks postpartum.
- Having GD increases your risk of developing Type 2 diabetes later in life. Maintaining a healthy lifestyle postpartum is critical.
