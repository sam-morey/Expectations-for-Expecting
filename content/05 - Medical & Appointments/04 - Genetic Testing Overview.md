# Genetic Testing Overview

Prenatal genetic testing helps parents understand if their baby has certain chromosomal or genetic conditions. It is optional — the decision to test involves medical, personal, and ethical considerations that are entirely individual.

---

## Why Consider Genetic Testing

- Information to prepare emotionally, logistically, and medically
- Opportunity to meet with specialists (MFM, genetic counselors) if needed
- Some conditions detected prenatally can benefit from specific birth hospital or immediate care plans
- Some parents want to know to make decisions about the pregnancy
- Some parents prefer not to know and will not change course regardless of results — this is a completely valid choice

---

## Conditions Most Commonly Screened For

### Chromosomal Trisomies
| Condition | Chromosomes | Prevalence | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|---|
| Trisomy 21 (Down syndrome) | Extra chromosome 21 | ~1/700 births | Intellectual disability; heart defects common; variable outcomes |
| Trisomy 18 (Edwards syndrome) | Extra chromosome 18 | ~1/5,000 | Severe; most not survive past first year |
| Trisomy 13 (Patau syndrome) | Extra chromosome 13 | ~1/10,000–20,000 | Severe; most not survive past first year |

**Trisomy 21 risk increases with maternal age:**
- Age 25: 1/1,250
- Age 30: 1/1,000
- Age 35: 1/400
- Age 40: 1/100
- Age 45: 1/30

### Sex Chromosome Abnormalities
- Turner syndrome (45,X): Females with one X chromosome; fertility issues
- Klinefelter syndrome (47,XXY): Males with extra X; mild; often not diagnosed until adulthood
- These can be detected on NIPT; providers vary on whether they include these in reports

### Neural Tube Defects
- Spina bifida, anencephaly
- Detectable via AFP (blood marker) and anatomy scan
- Folic acid supplementation before and early in pregnancy significantly reduces risk

### Microdeletion Syndromes (Extended NIPT)
- DiGeorge syndrome, Prader-Willi, Angelman, and others
- Detected by expanded NIPT panels
- Higher false positive rates than standard trisomy screening
- Discuss with provider or genetic counselor before ordering

---

## The Testing Pathway

```
Screening test (NIPT, NT scan, quad screen)
    ↓
Normal result → Continue routine care
    ↓
Abnormal / high-risk result
    ↓
Counseling with genetic counselor
    ↓
Diagnostic testing (amniocentesis or CVS) to confirm
    ↓
Results reviewed with MFM and/or genetics team
```

---

## Genetic Counseling

A genetic counselor is a healthcare professional specializing in hereditary conditions and prenatal risk. Valuable when:
- You have a family history of genetic conditions
- Carrier screening reveals a concern
- Screening tests return abnormal results
- You are navigating what the results mean and what to do next

Genetic counselors interpret risk without telling you what to decide — they provide information and support the decision-making process.

---

## Carrier Screening

Separate from fetal chromosomal testing — carrier screening tests parents for recessive genetic diseases.

| Condition | Population at Higher Risk |
|---|---|
| Cystic fibrosis | All populations; higher in Northern European |
| Spinal muscular atrophy (SMA) | All populations |
| Sickle cell disease | African, Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, South Asian |
| Tay-Sachs disease | Ashkenazi Jewish, French-Canadian |
| Fragile X (carrier: mother only) | Family history of intellectual disability |

Being a carrier generally means no personal health impact — but if both partners are carriers for the same condition, each pregnancy has a 25% chance of the condition (recessive inheritance).
