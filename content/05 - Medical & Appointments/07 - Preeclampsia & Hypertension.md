Preeclampsia is a serious blood pressure disorder that can happen during pregnancy or after childbirth. It is characterized by high blood pressure and signs of damage to another organ system, most often the liver and kidneys.

---

## Symptoms and Warning Signs

Preeclampsia can develop suddenly. Contact your provider immediately if you experience:
- **Severe headache** that doesn't go away with Tylenol.
- **Vision changes** (blurriness, seeing "spots," or light sensitivity).
- **Upper right abdominal pain** (under the ribs).
- **Sudden swelling** in the face, hands, or eyes (sudden weight gain of >2 lbs in a week).
- **Shortness of breath.**

---

## Types of Hypertensive Disorders

| Condition | Description |
|---|---|
| **Gestational Hypertension** | High blood pressure after 20 weeks without protein in urine or organ damage. |
| **Preeclampsia** | High blood pressure + protein in urine or organ damage. |
| **Preeclampsia with Severe Features** | Very high blood pressure (160/110+) or severe organ dysfunction; requires hospitalization. |
| **Eclampsia** | The onset of seizures in a person with preeclampsia; a medical emergency. |

---

## Treatment and Management

The only "cure" for preeclampsia is the delivery of the baby and placenta.
- **Aspirin:** Many women at risk are prescribed a daily low-dose aspirin starting in the second trimester.
- **Monitoring:** Frequent blood pressure checks at home and in the office, plus regular blood and urine tests.
- **Medication:** Blood pressure medications (like Labetalol) or magnesium sulfate (to prevent seizures) may be used.
- **Early Delivery:** Depending on the severity and your gestation, your provider may recommend an induction or cesarean as early as 34–37 weeks.

---

## Postpartum Preeclampsia

**Warning:** Preeclampsia can develop *after* you go home, most commonly within 48 hours but up to 6 weeks postpartum. Continue to monitor your blood pressure and watch for the warning signs above even after birth.
