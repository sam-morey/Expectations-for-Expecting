# Miscarriage

Miscarriage is the loss of a pregnancy before 20 weeks. It is the most common complication of early pregnancy — affecting roughly 10–20% of confirmed pregnancies — and far more common than most people realize before it happens to them. Understanding what it is, why it happens, and what comes next can reduce fear and help you respond clearly if it occurs.

---

## Types of Miscarriage

| Type | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **Threatened miscarriage** | Bleeding with a closed cervix; pregnancy may or may not continue |
| **Inevitable miscarriage** | Bleeding with an open cervix; loss cannot be stopped |
| **Incomplete miscarriage** | Some pregnancy tissue has passed but not all |
| **Complete miscarriage** | All pregnancy tissue has passed on its own |
| **Missed miscarriage** | Embryo has stopped developing but no symptoms have occurred; found on ultrasound |
| **Blighted ovum** | Fertilized egg implanted but never developed; gestational sac present, embryo not |
| **Chemical pregnancy** | Loss very early — often before a missed period; detected only by a positive test that becomes negative |
| **Recurrent miscarriage** | Three or more consecutive losses; warrants full evaluation |

---

## When Miscarriage Most Often Occurs

Most miscarriages happen in the first trimester — approximately 80% occur before week 12. Risk drops significantly after a heartbeat is confirmed and again at the end of the first trimester.

| Stage | Approximate Risk |
|---|---|
| Before heartbeat confirmed (~6 weeks) | 10–20% of confirmed pregnancies |
| After heartbeat confirmed | Drops to ~5% |
| After 12 weeks | ~1–2% |
| After 20 weeks (stillbirth, not miscarriage) | ~1 in 160 pregnancies |

---

## Signs & Symptoms

Symptoms vary depending on how early the loss occurs and the type. Some miscarriages are detected only on ultrasound with no symptoms at all (missed miscarriage).

**Common symptoms:**
- Vaginal bleeding — ranging from light spotting to heavy flow with clots
- Cramping or abdominal pain, often more intense than period cramps
- Lower back pain or pressure
- A sudden stop in pregnancy symptoms (nausea, breast tenderness)
- Tissue or clot-like material passing vaginally

**Important:** Light spotting is common in early pregnancy and does not always indicate miscarriage. Any bleeding warrants a call to your provider — they will determine next steps based on your situation.

---

## Causes

### The Most Common Cause: Chromosomal Abnormality

Approximately **50–70% of early miscarriages** are caused by random chromosomal errors during cell division. The embryo simply cannot develop normally. This is a biological quality-control mechanism — not a failure of the pregnancy, the mother, or anything she did.

These errors are typically one-time events and do not predict future pregnancies.

### Other Medical Causes

| Cause | Details |
|---|---|
| **Uterine abnormalities** | Fibroids, polyps, a septate or bicornuate uterus can interfere with implantation or growth |
| **Hormonal imbalances** | Low progesterone, thyroid disorders, or uncontrolled diabetes can affect the uterine environment |
| **Blood clotting disorders** | Antiphospholipid syndrome (APS) causes clots that disrupt placental circulation |
| **Incompetent cervix** | Cervix dilates without labor, typically in the second trimester; associated with late losses |
| **Infections** | Some untreated infections (listeria, certain STIs) can increase risk |
| **Immune disorders** | The immune system may in rare cases attack the pregnancy |

### What Does NOT Cause Miscarriage

This matters. Many people blame themselves after a loss.

- Exercise (even vigorous, in a healthy pregnancy)
- Sex
- Lifting or physical activity
- Stress or emotional distress
- A fall or minor bump
- Eating the "wrong" food on one occasion
- Having previously used birth control
- Morning sickness or lack of it

---

## Risk Factors

Some factors increase statistical risk. Having a risk factor does not mean a miscarriage will occur — it means the baseline probability is higher.

| Risk Factor | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Maternal age** | Most significant factor. Risk is ~10% at 20–30, ~20% at 35, ~40% at 40, ~80% at 45+ |
| **Previous miscarriage** | One prior loss increases risk slightly; recurrent loss (3+) warrants evaluation |
| **Uncontrolled chronic conditions** | Poorly managed diabetes, thyroid disease, autoimmune conditions |
| **Uterine or cervical abnormalities** | Often diagnosable and sometimes correctable |
| **Smoking** | Increases risk; dose-dependent |
| **Heavy alcohol use** | Avoid alcohol entirely during pregnancy |
| **High caffeine intake** | Evidence is mixed; most providers recommend staying under 200mg/day |
| **Obesity** | Moderately elevated risk, particularly for recurrent loss |
| **Paternal age** | Elevated paternal age (50+) modestly increases chromosomal error risk |

---

## Prevention

For chromosomal miscarriages — the majority — there is no prevention. They are random events. However, some causes of miscarriage are modifiable or treatable:

**Modifiable lifestyle factors:**
- Take a prenatal vitamin with folic acid before conception and throughout pregnancy
- Do not smoke; avoid secondhand smoke
- Avoid alcohol entirely during pregnancy
- Keep caffeine under 200mg/day
- Manage pre-existing conditions (diabetes, thyroid, autoimmune) with your provider before and during pregnancy

**Medical interventions (for diagnosed causes):**
- Progesterone supplementation may be prescribed for confirmed low progesterone
- Blood thinners (heparin) for diagnosed antiphospholipid syndrome
- Surgical correction of uterine abnormalities in some cases
- Cerclage (cervical stitch) for incompetent cervix, typically placed around 12–14 weeks

**If you've had recurrent losses:** Ask your provider for a recurrent pregnancy loss (RPL) workup, which screens for most treatable causes — chromosomal, anatomical, hormonal, immunological, and clotting factors.

---

## Diagnosis

Your provider will confirm a miscarriage through one or more of the following:

| Test | What It Shows |
|---|---|
| **Transvaginal ultrasound** | Confirms whether embryo/heartbeat is present; identifies missed miscarriage |
| **Serial hCG levels** | In a healthy pregnancy, hCG doubles every 48–72 hours; a plateau or drop is a concerning sign |
| **Pelvic exam** | Checks whether cervix is open or closed |
| **Tissue analysis** | If pregnancy tissue passes, it can be tested for chromosomal cause |

---

## Medical Management Options

If a miscarriage is confirmed, there are three approaches. There is no universally "right" option — your provider will discuss what's appropriate for your situation, and your preference matters.

| Option | What Happens | Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| **Expectant management** | Wait for the body to pass tissue naturally | Days to 2 weeks |
| **Medication (misoprostol)** | Medication taken orally or vaginally causes cramping and passage of tissue | Usually within 24–48 hours |
| **Surgical (D&C or MVA)** | Uterus evacuated in a brief procedure; recommended for incomplete miscarriage, heavy bleeding, or infection concern | Immediate; under 30 minutes |

All three are medically safe. Surgical management is faster and more complete; expectant management avoids a procedure but is less predictable in timing.

---

## Recovery

### Physical Recovery

- Light bleeding and cramping may continue for 1–2 weeks
- Most people get their next period within 4–6 weeks
- hCG levels return to zero within a few weeks (may take longer after later losses)
- Physically, most people can try to conceive again after one normal cycle, though some providers recommend waiting one full cycle for dating purposes

### Trying Again

- After a single miscarriage, most people go on to have a healthy subsequent pregnancy
- Recurrent miscarriage (3+) warrants a full workup before trying again
- There is no evidence that waiting longer (beyond one cycle) improves outcomes after a single loss
- Discussing timing with your provider based on your specific situation is always the right call

---

## Emotional Recovery

Miscarriage is a loss. Grief is normal — and it does not scale with gestational age. A loss at 6 weeks can be just as devastating as a later one. There is no right way to feel.

**What to expect:**
- Sadness, shock, guilt, anger, and anxiety about future pregnancies are all common
- Partners grieve too, often differently and less visibly
- Grief does not follow a timeline — it can resurface at due dates, subsequent pregnancies, or out of nowhere

**When to seek support:**
- If grief significantly interferes with daily life for more than a few weeks
- If anxiety about a subsequent pregnancy becomes overwhelming
- Perinatal mental health specialists are trained specifically in pregnancy loss — ask your provider for a referral
