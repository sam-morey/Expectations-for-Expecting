# Sleep During Pregnancy

Sleep is profoundly affected by pregnancy — from early hormonal changes through the physical discomforts of the third trimester. Understanding what's happening and what actually helps makes the difference between surviving the nights and managing them.

---

## Sleep Changes by Trimester

### First Trimester
- **Extreme sleepiness** is the dominant feature — progesterone is a powerful sedative
- Despite fatigue, sleep quality is often disrupted by nausea, heartburn, and frequent urination
- Total sleep need increases; most women need 8–10 hours
- **Strategy:** Rest when you can; napping is appropriate; this phase ends

### Second Trimester
- Most women's best sleep trimester
- Fatigue decreases; bump not yet large enough to cause physical discomfort
- Some light back pain, leg cramps, and vivid dreams begin
- Start establishing good sleep positioning habits now before third trimester

### Third Trimester
- Sleep becomes the most significant challenge of pregnancy
- Interrupted by: frequent urination, back and hip pain, Braxton Hicks, leg cramps, heartburn, fetal movement, anxiety, shortness of breath
- Most women wake 3–5+ times per night
- **Strategy:** Adjust expectations; total sleep is cumulative; nighttime rest even without sleep has value

---

## Sleep Position: Why Left Side Is Recommended

After ~20 weeks, sleeping on the back compresses the inferior vena cava (IVC) — the large vein returning blood to the heart. This can reduce blood flow to the uterus and placenta.

**Recommendation:** Left side sleeping  
**Why left:** Places the uterus away from the IVC; slightly improves blood flow compared to right side  
**Right side:** Also acceptable; not dangerous  
**On back:** Occasional rolling onto back during sleep is fine; deliberately sleeping on back for extended periods is what to avoid

**Realistic context:** Waking up on your back occasionally does not harm the baby. The body's natural response (discomfort, dizziness) typically causes a position shift. Don't panic if you wake up on your back — just roll to your side.

## Practical Sleep Solutions

### Pregnancy Pillow
The single most useful third trimester sleep purchase.

| Type | Description | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| Full-length U-shaped | Surrounds entire body; supports head, back, belly, and between knees | All-in-one support; doesn't require repositioning |
| C-shaped | Supports front and slightly behind | Belly and knee support |
| Wedge | Small; supports belly from underneath | Supplementing existing pillow setup |
| Regular pillow between knees | Budget option | Reducing hip and back pain |

### Managing Specific Sleep Disruptors

| Disruptor | Strategy |
|---|---|
| Frequent urination | Reduce fluids in last 1–2 hours before bed |
| Heartburn | Elevate upper body (wedge or propped pillows); avoid eating 2–3 hours before bed |
| Leg cramps | Stretch calves before bed; magnesium glycinate 200–350mg at night; ensure calcium intake |
| Back and hip pain | Pillow between knees; semi-reclined position if flat is uncomfortable |
| Shortness of breath | Slightly propped-up upper body; improves once baby drops |
| Anxiety / racing mind | See [[02 - Anxiety & Prenatal Worry]]; journaling before bed; limit news/screens |
| Vivid dreams | Normal; progesterone and sleep cycle changes; not harmful |
| Braxton Hicks | Hydrate; change position; usually resolve with rest |

## Napping

Napping is beneficial when nighttime sleep is severely disrupted. Key considerations:
- Keep naps to 20–30 minutes to avoid deep sleep and nighttime interference
- If exhausted, longer naps (60–90 minutes) are sometimes necessary and fine
- First trimester: nap without guilt; the fatigue is real

---

## When Sleep Problems Become a Medical Issue

**Talk to your provider if:**
- Snoring loudly or waking gasping for breath — possible pregnancy-related sleep apnea (risk increases in third trimester, especially with excess weight gain)
- Restless leg syndrome (RLS) — creeping, crawling sensations in legs at rest; treatable
- Insomnia significantly affecting functioning — medication options exist (safe options: unisom, melatonin short-term; discuss with provider)
