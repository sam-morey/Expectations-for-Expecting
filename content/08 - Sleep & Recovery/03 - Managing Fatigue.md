Fatigue during pregnancy is not ordinary tiredness — it is a physiological state driven by massive hormonal and metabolic changes. Understanding why it happens, when it's worst, and how to manage it reduces the frustration of feeling so tired when everything looks fine from the outside.

---

## Why Pregnancy Causes Fatigue

### First Trimester Fatigue
- **Progesterone** is the primary driver — it has sedative properties and rises dramatically in early pregnancy
- The body is building the placenta — an entirely new organ — which is enormously energy-intensive
- Blood pressure is often lower (vasodilation); cardiac output is increasing
- Blood volume is expanding; some anemia common before iron stores fully ramp up
- hCG levels are peaking; directly associated with fatigue in some research
- Emotional and psychological adjustment also depletes energy reserves

**Result:** Profound, sometimes incapacitating fatigue in weeks 6–10. Many women need 9–10 hours of sleep and still feel tired.

### Second Trimester
- Fatigue typically resolves significantly as the placenta becomes fully functional (~weeks 12–16)
- Energy returns; this is the most comfortable trimester for most women
- If fatigue persists, check iron levels — iron deficiency anemia is common

### Third Trimester Fatigue
- Fatigue returns as the body carries increasing weight
- Sleep quality degrades (see [[01 - Sleep During Pregnancy]])
- Organ compression (diaphragm, stomach) makes breathing and eating harder
- Emotional intensity of late pregnancy; birth preparation; anxiety
- Body is in full-scale preparation mode for labor

## Practical Fatigue Management

### Permission to Rest
The most effective intervention for first and third trimester fatigue is rest. This is not laziness — it is a physiological response to building and sustaining a human life.

**If you can:** Reduce workload, especially in weeks 6–12 and weeks 34–40.  
**If you can't:** Protect sleep hours; delegate non-essential tasks; communicate needs to partner and support people.

### Iron Levels
Iron deficiency anemia is one of the most common and most treatable causes of fatigue in pregnancy. Symptoms: exhaustion, pale gums/inner eyelids, shortness of breath, brain fog.

- CBC and iron panel are done at the first prenatal appointment and often again in the third trimester
- If anemic: iron supplementation (ferrous bisglycinate or ferrous sulfate); pair with vitamin C; avoid calcium at same meal

### Blood Sugar Stability
Unstable blood sugar worsens fatigue. Strategies:
- Eat protein + fat + complex carbohydrate at each meal
- Avoid long gaps between meals (>4–5 hours)
- Carry a protein-rich snack

### Hydration
Dehydration directly causes fatigue and worsens every pregnancy symptom. Target 8–10 cups/day; carry water everywhere.

### Exercise (Counterintuitively)
Moderate exercise improves pregnancy energy levels by improving circulation and sleep quality. A 20–30 minute walk often increases afternoon energy. Exercise fatigue (normal post-workout tiredness) is different from pregnancy fatigue and recovers quickly.

### Realistic Expectations
First trimester: survive it. The fatigue is temporary and peaks before most people know you're even pregnant, making it feel isolating. It ends.

Third trimester: adjust expectations for productivity and physical capacity. The body is doing an enormous amount of work invisibly.

## When Fatigue Is a Warning Sign

Contact your provider if fatigue is accompanied by:
- Shortness of breath at rest
- Palpitations
- Severe paleness
- Dizziness or fainting
- Persistent fatigue despite adequate sleep and good nutrition (rule out thyroid dysfunction, severe anemia)
