# Budgeting for Baby

A practical framework for understanding and managing the financial transition of adding a child.

---

## Step 1: Know Your Current Baseline

Before projecting baby costs, establish your current baseline:
- Monthly take-home income (both partners)
- Fixed monthly expenses (rent/mortgage, car, insurance, subscriptions)
- Variable monthly spending (food, entertainment, clothing, misc)
- Current savings rate
- Emergency fund status

## Savings Strategies

### Before Baby Arrives
- Redirect pregnancy-related savings (maternity clothes, nursery) back to savings after purchases
- Use registry + shower gifts to offset gear costs substantially
- Build the leave buffer above your normal emergency fund

### After Baby Arrives
- Dependent Care FSA: $5,000/year in pre-tax dollars for childcare; reduces taxable income
- Compare daycare tax credit vs. FSA with your tax professional (most families benefit more from FSA)
- 529 college savings plan: Even $25–50/month started at birth compounds significantly over 18 years

---

## Mental Model: Absorb vs. Offset

Most families don't "find" money for baby costs — they trade other spending categories for childcare and baby expenses.

Common tradeoffs families make:
- Reduce dining out / entertainment: $300–$600/month
- Reduce personal discretionary spending: $200–$400/month
- Pause aggressive retirement contributions temporarily (evaluate carefully)
- Delay large purchases (cars, home improvements, vacations)

Childcare is the dominant variable. The decision about childcare type — nanny, daycare, in-home care, one parent staying home — is the single largest financial lever available.
