# Childcare Costs & Planning

Childcare is typically the largest new expense a family faces. Planning early is critical — quality infant care has waitlists of 6–18 months in most cities.

---

## Childcare Options Compared

| Option | Average Monthly Cost | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| Full-time daycare center (infant) | $1,200–$3,500 | Regulated; structured; social; backup staff if caregiver sick | Less flexible; waitlists; requires drop-off/pickup |
| In-home daycare (family daycare) | $800–$2,000 | Smaller group; often more flexible; typically lower cost | Less regulated; closed when provider sick or on vacation |
| Nanny (full-time) | $3,000–$6,000 | 1:1 care; flexible; in your home; schedule customizable | Most expensive; sole caregiver risk; employment taxes |
| Nanny share (shared nanny) | $1,800–$3,500 | Reduced cost vs. solo nanny; more social for baby | Requires coordinating with another family; shared schedule |
| Au pair | $950–$1,500 (stipend) | Cost-effective for multiple children; cultural exchange | Must provide room/board; limited to 45 hrs/week; 12-month commitment |
| Family care (grandparent, relative) | $0–$600 (support) | Cost savings; trusted caregivers | Depends on family's situation and proximity; may complicate relationships |
| One parent stays home | Childcare cost = $0; lost income varies | No childcare cost; parental care; flexible | Significant income loss; career interruption; no adult backup |

## Geographic Cost Variation

Infant care costs vary enormously by location:

| Area | Average Full-Time Infant Care |
|---|---|
| San Francisco | $2,800–$4,500/month |
| New York City | $2,500–$4,000/month |
| Seattle | $2,200–$3,500/month |
| Chicago | $1,800–$2,800/month |
| Dallas | $1,200–$1,800/month |
| Rural/Midwest average | $700–$1,400/month |

## The Waitlist Problem

**Infant care (0–12 months) is the most expensive and most waitlisted** category of childcare. Demand for infant spots far exceeds supply in most areas.

**Timeline:**
- Start researching: 2nd trimester (weeks 14–20)
- Tour facilities: 2nd–3rd trimester
- Join waitlists: Immediately upon touring; before birth
- **Expect waitlists of 6–18 months** at quality centers in major cities
- If you don't get your first choice, maintain multiple waitlists and keep checking

**What to do now:** Even if your due date is months away, start this process.

## Choosing a Childcare Provider

### For Daycare Centers
- [ ] Licensed and accredited (NAEYC accreditation is a quality marker)
- [ ] Infant-to-caregiver ratio (4:1 or better for infants; check state requirements)
- [ ] Staff tenure and turnover (low turnover = more consistent caregivers)
- [ ] Philosophy alignment (how they handle feeding, sleep, soothing)
- [ ] Communication style (daily reports, app, photos)
- [ ] Sick policy; backup when center staff is sick
- [ ] Security (who can pick up; entry systems)

### For Nannies
- [ ] Background check and references (always)
- [ ] CPR certification
- [ ] Experience with infants specifically
- [ ] Trial period (paid) before full commitment
- [ ] Employment agreement covering hours, pay, PTO, sick time, backup plan
- [ ] Nanny payroll and taxes (household employer; check IRS requirements and tools like HomePay)

## Childcare Tax Savings

| Benefit | Amount | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Dependent Care FSA | $5,000/year pre-tax | Reduces taxable income; saves 22–37% in taxes on $5K |
| Child and Dependent Care Tax Credit | 20–35% of up to $3,000 ($1 child) or $6,000 (2+) | Credit, not deduction; may be less than FSA benefit |
| Employer childcare benefits | Varies | Some employers offer additional childcare subsidies |

**In most cases, the Dependent Care FSA is more valuable than the CDCTC for middle and higher earners.** Consult a tax professional.
